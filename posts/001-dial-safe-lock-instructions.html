Safe Dial Lock Instructions: A Guide for Every Brand

Dial lock instructions vary depending on brand, lock type, and high-security features. Here's how to open a Sargent & Greenleaf, LaGard, and Kaba Mas dial safe lock the right way — every time.

If you own a safe with a mechanical dial combination lock, you already know it's one of the most reliable locking mechanisms ever made. Unlike electronic locks, dial locks don't depend on batteries, circuit boards, or keypad membranes that can fail over time.

But here's the catch — dial lock instructions vary significantly by brand and model. A Sargent & Greenleaf lock doesn't spin the same way as a LaGard. And a high-security Kaba Mas lock has its own unique procedure. Entering the wrong sequence or applying too much pressure can leave you locked out or, worse, damage the lock mechanism.

In this guide, we'll break down the correct dialing procedure for the three most common safe lock brands found in the Sacramento area and across the United States.

Before You Start: The Golden Rules

Regardless of which brand of lock you have, these principles apply universally:

  • Turn the dial slowly and deliberately. Rushing leads to overshooting numbers.
  • Stop exactly on the number. Not between numbers — the wheels inside align only at exact positions.
  • Never force the handle while dialing. If the handle doesn't move, you haven't completed the sequence correctly.
  • Know your lock's drop-in point. Most locks engage (drop) when the dial is turned left after the final number.
  • Count turns carefully. The first number requires more spins than you think on many locks.
⚠️ Warning: If your safe has high-security features like a relocking device or hardened plate, improper dialing can trigger the relocker — which requires a professional safe technician to reset. When in doubt, call a pro.

Sargent & Greenleaf (S&G) Dial Locks

Sargent & Greenleaf is the most common brand of mechanical safe lock in the United States. They're found on everything from home gun safes to bank vaults. The standard S&G lock uses a 3-number combination and requires a specific 4-step sequence.

S&G Standard 4-Step Sequence

Combination example: 25 — 86 — 50 (4-turn lock)

  1. Start at 0. Turn the dial LEFT (counterclockwise), passing the first number THREE times, then stop on it the fourth time.
    Left past 25 → Left past 25 → Left past 25 → Stop on 25
  2. Turn RIGHT (clockwise) past the second number TWO times, then stop on it the third time.
    Right past 86 → Right past 86 → Stop on 86
  3. Turn LEFT (counterclockwise) past the third number ONE time, then stop on it the second time.
    Left past 50 → Stop on 50
  4. Turn RIGHT (clockwise) slowly until the dial stops. This is the "drop" — the lock bolt is now retracted. Turn the handle to open.
💡 Pro tip: Some S&G locks are "3-turn" locks. For these, spin left 3 times past the first number (landing on it the 4th time), right 2 times past the second (land on it the 3rd), left 1 time past the third. The procedure is the same; the nomenclature just differs on what's called a "turn."

LaGard Dial Locks

LaGard (now part of Kaba Mas) is another major manufacturer of safe locks. LaGard locks have a slightly different feel compared to S&G — they often require less force to turn and have a smoother dial action.

LaGard Standard 3-Number Sequence

Combination example: 32 — 78 — 15

  1. Turn the dial LEFT (counterclockwise) 4 times and stop on the first number (32) on the 4th rotation.
  2. Turn RIGHT (clockwise) 3 times and stop on the second number (78) on the 3rd rotation.
  3. Turn LEFT (counterclockwise) 2 times and stop on the third number (15) on the 2nd rotation.
  4. Turn RIGHT (clockwise) slowly until the dial stops. Pull or turn the handle to open.
🔧 Note: LaGard locks are known for their smooth "drop." Some LaGard models use a 2-number sequence or include additional security features like a relocking trigger. Check your safe's manual if available.

Kaba Mas High-Security Locks

Kaba Mas (formerly Mas-Hamilton) makes some of the most secure mechanical locks in the world. These locks are found on high-value commercial safes, ATMs, and government vaults. Dialing a Kaba Mas lock requires absolute precision.

Kaba Mas 3-Number Sequence

Combination example: 45 — 20 — 85

  1. Turn LEFT 4 times and stop on 45. Make sure you count precisely — Kaba Mas wheels are tight.
  2. Turn RIGHT 3 times and stop on 20.
  3. Turn LEFT 2 times and stop on 85.
  4. Turn RIGHT until the dial stops. The handle should now turn freely.
⚠️ Critical: Many Kaba Mas locks include a "change key" port on the back of the lock. If you feel unusual resistance while dialing, stop immediately. Forcing a Kaba Mas lock can permanently damage the internal wheel pack, requiring full replacement.

Common Mistakes That Lock People Out

As a safe technician serving the Sacramento area, here are the most common dialing mistakes I see:

  • Not counting rotations correctly. The most frequent error. If you spin left 3 times when you needed 4, the wheels won't align.
  • Stopping BETWEEN numbers. The dial markers on the ring are for alignment — the internal wheels engage only at exact number positions.
  • Dialing the wrong direction on the final step. Always turn RIGHT slowly to find the drop point.
  • Using an old, worn combination. If your safe is used or secondhand, the combination may have been changed at some point. Always verify with the original documentation or previous owner.
  • Applying handle pressure while dialing. This binds the bolt and prevents the lock from dropping. Keep the handle in neutral position until the drop occurs.

What If Your Dial Lock Won't Open?

If you've followed the correct sequence for your brand and the handle still won't move, here's what might be wrong:

  • The combination was changed and not recorded. This happens surprisingly often — especially with inherited safes or business transitions.
  • Internal lock failure. Springs break, wheel pins wear, and debris can jam the mechanism over decades of use.
  • The relocking device has activated. This is triggered by attempted drilling or forced entry — or sometimes just by old age and vibration.
  • You have the wrong combination sequence. Some locks use a reverse sequence or different number of turns.

Dial Lock Maintenance Tips

Mechanical dial locks can last for decades with minimal maintenance. Here's how to keep yours working:

  • Lubricate sparingly. Use a dry graphite lubricant — never oil or WD-40, which attract dust and gum up the wheels.
  • Dial the combination at least once a month. Even if you don't open the safe, running the wheels keeps them from sticking.
  • Keep the dial area clean. Dust and debris can work their way into the spindle hole over time.
  • Have your lock serviced every 5-10 years. A professional cleaning and inspection can prevent failures before they happen.

Dial locks are a proven, reliable technology — but like any mechanical device, they need understanding and occasional care. Whether you're opening your safe for the first time or troubleshooting a stubborn lock, the right procedure makes all the difference.
